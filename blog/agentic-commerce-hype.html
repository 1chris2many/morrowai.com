Reacting to The Batch May 2026

The Agentic Commerce Hype Cycle: Where We Actually Are

By Chris Morrow

I've been reading the agentic AI coverage closely — Andrew Ng's The Batch has been especially good on this — and I keep having the same reaction: the vision is right, but the timeline is wrong.

Let me share where I actually think we are.

First, Let's Agree on What "Agentic Commerce" Actually Means

Not a chatbot. Not an LLM with an "add to cart" button. Real agentic commerce means an AI that can autonomously discover, evaluate, negotiate, and purchase on your behalf — without you approving each step.

You tell it: "Get me the best running shoes for under $150 that'll arrive before Saturday." You walk away. It handles the rest.

That's the vision. And it's a genuinely compelling one. Andy Jassy said it plainly: "We are very bullish on what agentic commerce will look like." I agree with him. I just think we're all underestimating how long the road is.

Where We Actually Are Right Now

Early agentic features exist. I know this firsthand — I worked on Amazon Rufus, which now has things like price tracking, auto-buy triggers on price drops, and voice reordering for tens of millions of users.

Here's the key thing about those features: they work because they're constrained.

The agent isn't making judgment calls. It's executing rules you already set. "If this item drops below $40, buy it." That's not autonomous decision-making. That's a fancy if/then statement with a nice UI on top.

The demos that are blowing up on Twitter? Most of them are that. A constrained workflow with an LLM wrapper calling it "agentic." The tech looks impressive. But it's a long way from "go handle my holiday shopping."

The Trust Gap Nobody Has Solved

Here's the question I ask everyone who pitches me on agentic commerce: Would you let an AI spend $500 on your behalf without confirmation? What about $5,000?

Watch people squirm.

This isn't a technology problem. The AI quality is getting there faster than anyone expected. The problem is human psychology — and behind that, legal liability.

When an agent buys the wrong thing, who owns it? The user who authorized the agent? The platform that deployed it? The model provider? Right now, nobody has a clean answer. Existing consumer protection law was written assuming a human clicked "buy." It doesn't cleanly map onto an autonomous system acting on your behalf.

Until that liability question is resolved — through case law, regulation, or explicit contractual frameworks — enterprise adoption is going to be slow. And consumer adoption even slower. People will tolerate an AI suggesting a product. They will not tolerate an AI draining their checking account on a bad call.

The Unsexy Blocker: Payment Authorization

Everyone talks about AI quality. Nobody talks about payment infrastructure, and it's the more interesting problem.

Every payment system in existence was built around human authentication. Credit card networks, bank fraud detection, Stripe, bank APIs — they assume a human is present at the point of transaction. The behavioral signals fraud models look for (typing speed, mouse movement, device fingerprint) don't exist when an agent executes a purchase.

Rebuilding that infrastructure for autonomous agents isn't a feature sprint. It's a multi-year industry coordination problem involving card networks, banks, regulators, and platform providers. It's genuinely hard. And it's boring, which is why it doesn't show up in pitch decks.

What Smart Investors Are Actually Asking

I do a lot of investor due diligence work around agentic commerce. The quality of questions has gotten a lot better in the last 18 months.

The best investors aren't asking about features. They're asking about failure modes. What happens when the agent gets it wrong? What's the recourse? What's the liability exposure? How does the system handle edge cases at scale?

The smart money right now is going into picks and shovels — evaluation frameworks, guardrails, trust infrastructure, agent observability tooling. Not the flashy consumer-facing agent demos. The infrastructure layer is where durable value gets built, and it's still early.

The Realistic 3-5 Year View

Here's my actual framework for thinking about timelines:

18-24 months: Narrow-scope agents work reliably. Subscription management, reordering known products, price optimization within pre-authorized limits. These will go mainstream because they're low-risk and high-value. The trust and liability questions are manageable when the scope is tight.

3-5 years: Mid-complexity agents. "Find me a birthday gift under $200 for my dad who likes fishing." This requires real judgment, but in a bounded category with human confirmation before purchase.

5+ years (probably): Fully autonomous broad-scope agents. The "just handle my shopping" use case. This requires solved liability frameworks, rebuilt payment infrastructure, and a level of demonstrated trust that takes years to earn at scale.

Anyone showing you a pitch deck with broad autonomous agents in production at scale in 18 months is either lying to you or lying to themselves.

The Bottom Line

I believe in agentic commerce. I've spent years building toward it. The vision Jassy and others are articulating is correct.

But demos aren't products. The tech working in a controlled environment is not the same as the tech working for 50 million people with real money, real fraud, and real legal exposure.

The builders and investors who are going to win are the ones who take the vision seriously and take the hard problems seriously. Trust, liability, and payment authorization aren't obstacles that will magically get solved when the models get better. They require deliberate, unglamorous work.

Do that work now. Everyone else is going to be surprised by how much it matters.
